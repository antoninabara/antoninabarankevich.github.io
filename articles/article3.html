La Raza by Frost

The musical track of “La Raza” is laid-back and “suave” thanks to its moderate tempo and smooth saxophone inviting to groove and lounge. The song’s message is self-affirming and powerful. Behind the self-righteous machismo and images of guns, there is a need for empowerment and respect. According to Oliver Wang, “Frost’s use of hip hop as a vehicle to deliver his Chicano pride manifesto affirms identity while simultaneously bridging communities.” Sampling “Viva Tirado” reveals the long history of alliances between Latino and African-American musicians. Although there have been tensions possibly perpetrated by the government, both communities have struggled through similar hazards like discrimination and structural violence. According to Oliver Wang, “Viva Tirado” was an anthem to the Chicano movement, while Frost’s “La Raza” became a hymn of his generation. 

One source of the song’s force is in its title and in the most dominant word “la Raza”. It is serendipitous that the word “La Raza” with its rolling “erre” and the buzzing [s], is itself sharp and heavy. When Kid Frost enunciates it, “La Raza” produces a soundwave that cuts through the air like a razor blade of a machete. 

 Juggling two languages requires extra versatility. The way a person belonging to two cultures balances languages may indicate the degree to which these cultures dominate their mind. In “La Raza” Kid Frost mixes and rhymes English and Spanish creating his personal version of calo. It is interesting how sometimes he chooses to use English grammatical features to frame Spanish words:

Cruising in the calle, headed for the bolo

Mexican-Americans who came from Mexico might prefer to say “Cruising en la calle, headed por el bolo”, using Spanish articles and preposition since they also perfectly fit into the rhythm. Mexican-Americans who were born in the US might be more comfortable using English grammar with the Spanish vocabulary. Hybrid languages like calo are particularly flexible, non-conformist and very individual. Being rightful heirs of both American and Mexican cultures, Chicanos draw from both languages to create their unique version of the dialect.
